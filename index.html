A Brief Analysis

Intersections have the complex task of managing and moving people and machines travelling in many directions at varying velocities. It is not an inherently visual system.

A class-action lawsuit was filed in June 2018 against New York City by the Disability Rights Advocates. It claimed that the lack of APS at 96.6% of city intersections violates the Americans with Disabilities Act. On July 22, 2019, the court ruled that the near-total absence at the Citys signalized intersections of crossing information accessible to blind and low vision pedestrians denies such persons meaningful access to these intersections.

APS is citywide infrastructure. It is also a sound. It is not just a button pushed for sound but an active subject in the city's urban fabric. The chirp from the 10-watt speaker is as intrinsic to an intersection as the colour of the traffic lights.

Object Artifact 1 - 880Hz

A 3 minute audio file looking at the sound of an APS.



Transcript

In 1976, a study determined that the best frequency for an accessible pedestrian signal or APS is between 300 Hertz and 1000 Hertz. It was also determined that a square wave, which allows the amplitude to alternate at a steady frequency between its minimum and maximum values was the easiest to hear above background street noise.

Here's an example of a square wave with a frequency of 300 Hertz.

Here's an example of a square wave with a frequency of 1000 Hertz.

In the end, it was determined that a square wave with a frequency of 880 Hertz was the optimal sound profile for an APS. Here's what that sounds like.

Let's listen to these sounds. When played alongside the sounds of a busy New York street. First, you will hear 300 Hertz and then 1000 Hertz and finally 880 Hertz.

A 1997 study determined the mean intensity of accelerating traffic from the point of view of a waiting pedestrian is 89 decibels. Currently the Federal Highway Administration's Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices permits a maximum volume of an APS to be 89 decibel. For example, the top volume of an iPhone is 102 decibels. At 80% volume an iPhone plays at 89 decibels.

The sounds played alongside New York City streets were set to similar volumes. A 1999 study found that in order to reach 90% intelligibility, a sound has to be 15 decibels louder than background noise. Here is 880 Hertz playing 15 decibels louder than the city streets.

Finally, here's one of the recommended APS walk indicators playing 15 decibels louder than the city streets.

Object Artifact 2 - Materiality

An APS is both the object and the subject in the public sphere. As described in Object Artifact 1 - 880 Hz, the APS is the subject of the ears of passing pedestrians and those using it to navigate city streets. For an APS to work as intended, someone must firmly press its button. Without the pedestrian acting upon the APS, it would chirp endlessly once every second. 880Hz was determined to be intelligible but not irritating, but what can we say about the touch of the arrow button?

The button' is made of aluminum that is nickel-plated and powder coated. Aluminum is commonly used in manufacturing as it is lightweight and has good corrosion-resistant properties. Nickle plating is a further measure used to protect aluminum from corrosion and ensures its durability in harsh environments. Nickel plating is a process used in the automotive and aeronautics industries for parts found in engines and turbines.

Nickel plating on the button of an APS guarantees it will remain functional over several years and thousands of pushes. The skin reads the texture, weight, density and temperature of matter (Pallasmaa 62). There is no doubt that the button is cold to the touch. The button is made to be pushed, not to be touched.

The sound of the APS is collective, experienced by everyone standing on the street corner and beyond. One person acts upon the APS at a time; the touch of the APS is solitary. Is this why the haptic perception of the APS is sequestered to durability and function (being pressed). If credibility is given to the negative qualities of the sound wave, why is the same not extended to the nickel-plated aluminum?
